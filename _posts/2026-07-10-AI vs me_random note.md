Yet as I kept writing, it turned into a self-reflection.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The hour hand points to four in the afternoon, yet I still stare blankly at the computer screen. Overwhelmed by distress and restlessness, my fingers on the keyboard feel as heavy as lead. The infamous "writer's block on thesis writing" has finally caught up with me today.

I gaze at the thesis I’ve been revising for half a year on the screen, where the blinking cursor beside the title acts like a spectator sneering at my plight.

"How should I revise this?"
"What new angles can I possibly come up with?"

These two questions have lingered in my mind for six months, swirling around hundreds of times each day only to loop back to square one.

Thankfully, we live in an era where AI tools are readily accessible.

![图片](/images/260710_1.jpg)

Now it only takes one minute to fully comprehend an article: drag the PDF file into the chat box, add a prompt like "Explain this to me word for word as if I were an elementary school student", and the screen will instantly fill with an analysis more thorough than my middle school reference textbooks.

No longer do I have to spend three grueling days poring over a single English research paper, rereading it over and over yet barely grasping anything beyond the abstract, as I did in my first year of postgraduate studies. Those tough days seem to have been erased with a single click.

Every morning, my worry has shifted from "How can I understand this paper?" to "Which AI should I use today?" — Doubao, DeepSeek, ChatGPT, or Gemini?

I have even generously subscribed to memberships for several AI models, feeling like an academic emperor with a whole harem at my disposal—picking and choosing, dividing my attention evenly among all of them.

Every now and then, a jolt of realization hits me: I need to go through the content myself. What if the AI deceives me?

Yet ninety percent of the time, this unease gets crushed by an even stronger sense of trust—these models are a hundred million times smarter than I am. They anticipate every thought I can come up with, and fill in all the gaps I fail to notice. They can even rework an entire piece to match the tone I suddenly decide on, breaking down complex ideas and serving them up to me effortlessly.

**I truly have become that academic emperor.**

But even emperors grow weary.

The text generated by these tools all starts to blur into the same bland template: polished, comprehensive, flawless, yet utterly lacking edge and personality. As I read on, aesthetic fatigue washes over me like a tide. I catch myself complaining: "Why can’t it just draft a Nature paper for me straight away?"

![图片](/images/260710_2.jpg)

The thought strikes me as absurd the moment it crosses my mind, yet I cannot help entertaining it.

Rewind to earlier that day, around 16:35 in the afternoon. I was staring at Chinese text on Google Translate when my random playlist suddenly switched to Queen’s Bohemian Rhapsody. The moment the dynamic, dramatically charged piano melodies and operatic recitatives flooded my ears, my heart skipped a beat.

*🎶Nothing really matters🎶*

Then a thought surfaced out of nowhere:
I haven’t opened my PDF reader or Zhiyun Translation for… well over half a year.

**Not just "rarely used"—not once at all.**

The last time I read an academic paper entirely on my own was during Professor Yang Li's writing class. He stood right behind me watching my screen, and I was too embarrassed to pull up an AI tool in front of him, so I forced myself to translate the text manually and handed in the assignment. Since then, I have never tackled a research paper by myself again.

I think I finally understand why my own manuscript reads so lifeless and stagnant.

It is not that GPT fails to pull up enough online literature, 

​	nor that Doubao provides shallow analysis,

​			nor that any AI model lacks intelligence. 

The truth is, I have not sat down to thoroughly read even two research papers in peace for half a year. I can barely articulate the core arguments I want to make, yet I expect AI to sort everything out for me.

There was a line on the slides from Professor Ming Tang's writing workshop that I paid no mind to back then, yet it boomerangs back to sting me now:

![图片](/images/260710_3.jpg)

Over these past several months, all I have ever asked AI to do is "find several papers that back up my argument." Naturally, the tools obediently retrieve piles of supporting evidence, every single piece aligned perfectly with my existing logic.

Trapped in this cozy self-reinforcing loop, my research paper spins its wheels without progress—no intellectual friction, no unexpected discoveries, no genuine academic growth.

***Technology lets us process information faster, yet it fails to help us engage with it more deeply.***
